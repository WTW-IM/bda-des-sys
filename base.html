Typography

Body copy

The Benefits Delivery & Administration UI Toolkit uses Source Sans Pro which was chosen because of its weights, character sets, legibility, and pleasantness. Our fallback font stack satisfies similar goals as well (Segoe UI, Segoe, Calibri, Tahoma, sans-serif). We use the "Regular" font for body text, the "Bold" font for bolded itens (e.g.: <strong>), and the "Light" font for headings.

Global default font-size is 18px, with a line-height of 1.428. This is applied to the <body> and all <p> tags (paragraphs). In addition, paragraphs receive a bottom margin of half their computed line-height (10px by default).

Accessibility Callout

Italics and legibility

Studies have shown that italicized text presents significant legibility issues for those with dyslexia. For this reason, we have chosen to not use italics.

Links

Links that take the user to another spot on the page—or another page entirely—are underlined when appearing within a larger body of text, with no underline on :hover. Contextual help (popover) links have a dotted line, which goes to solid on :hover. Also note the cursor when hovering over these links. Contxtual help links maintain this pattern, regardless of context (within a body of text or stand-alone, etc.).

Voice & Tone

Our distinct voice and tone make messaging recognizable. It helps our participants understand who we are, what services we provide, and how we can help them.

All BDA communications should strive for uniformity in voice and tone across all participant communication channels.

Our tone of voice (when speaking to participants) can be encapsulated with the following passage:

Via Benefits has the experience, knowledge, and ability to help you through the process of managing and maintaining your health care coverage. We sincerely want to make the process as straightforward and simple as possible. We are your advisers and advocates, guiding you through the complexities of the health care marketplace.

The BDA voice and tone are:

  • Warm and friendly without being too casual
  • Active, confident, instructional
    • Wherever possible, replace indirect statements (you may, you might, it could be good if) with strong, positive statements (you can, you have the option to, you can choose to). We want to provide a language of agency so the reader understands the options.
  • Professional and authoritative; knowledgeable
  • Neutral and unbiased
  • Clear and concise
    • We strive to keep the language at a 5th grade reading level, with the addition of health care terminology (e.g., deductible, copay, coinsurance)
  • Empathetic and understanding
  • Trustworthy

The BDA voice and tone are not:

  • Boastful
    • Avoid using superlatives (e.g., best, greatest)
  • Condescending or bossy
  • Flippant or casual
  • Cold and removed, overly “corporate”
  • Full of slang or industry jargon
    • If necessary, add a definition that is clear and easily understood. Make no assumptions about what you think people should know or understand implicitly. Avoid acronyms (without explanation).
  • Passive or vague
    • If you need a quick tutorial on passive voice and the avoidance thereof, take a look at this tutorial on WikiHow.
  • Subjective or biased toward an insurance company or a type of people (e.g., people who are more computer savvy)

Color Contrast

Accessibility Callout

Always check contrast

When choosing colors for content on our sites, it is important o keep contrast in mind. Our accessitiblity standards dictate that we need to maintain a contrast ratio of 4.5:1 for "normal" text, and 3:1 for "large" text (18px or higher; 14px or higher, if bolded). Some of the WTW colors don't meet this criterion. For example: that's why the Blue, Green, and Gold Branded Alternative Buttons use @wtwGray for the text within the button.

Some analysis has been done on the brand palette, and the grayscale palette, using WebAIM's Color Contrast Checker tool. The results are presented here for quick reference.
